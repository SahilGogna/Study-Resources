# 3 Resume Mistakes Canadian Recruiters Reject — And the AI Prompt to Fix Each One

---

## How to Use This Guide

For each mistake below you will find the exact prompt to copy paste into ChatGPT or Claude. Replace the bracketed sections with your actual content. Read the output critically — AI suggestions are a starting point, not a final draft. You know your experience better than the model does.

For best results use Claude or GPT-4 and above. Paste your full resume text and the full job description directly into the prompt.

## Mistake 1 — Generic Summary

### What It Looks Like

"Hardworking professional with 3 years of experience seeking a challenging role in data analytics."

This appears in thousands of resumes. Recruiters skip it entirely. It says nothing about who you are, what you are good at, or why this company should care.

### Why It Gets You Rejected

Your summary is the first thing a recruiter reads after your name. A generic summary signals that you did not bother to tailor your application. It also wastes the most valuable real estate on your resume.

### The AI Prompt to Fix It

> Here is my background: [2 to 3 sentences about your experience, skills, and career stage]
>
> Here is the role I am applying for: [paste job title and company name]
>
> Here is the job description: [paste full job description]
>
> 1. Identify every technical skill, tool, methodology, and qualification mentioned in the job description
>
> Write a 3-sentence professional summary for my resume that:
>
> 1. Opens with my most relevant experience and years in the field
> 2. Highlights 2 to 3 specific skills that directly match this role
> 3. Closes with what I bring to this specific company or team
>
> Make it specific to this company, not generic. Do not use phrases like "dynamic professional", "passionate about data", or "hardworking."

### What Good Looks Like

Before: "Hardworking data analyst with 3 years of experience looking for new opportunities."

After: "Data Analyst with 3 years of experience transforming raw data into business decisions at a Canadian financial institution. Proficient in SQL, Python, and Power BI with a track record of reducing reporting time by 60%. Excited to bring that same rigour to Scotiabank's Digital Factory team."

## Mistake 2 — Responsibilities Instead of Achievements

### What It Looks Like

"Responsible for managing data pipelines."

"Helped the team with data analysis tasks."

"Worked on improving reporting processes."

These bullets describe your job description, not your impact. Every other candidate applying for the same role has the same responsibilities.

### Why It Gets You Rejected

Recruiters spend 6 to 10 seconds on your resume. Bullets that describe duties are forgettable. Bullets that describe results are memorable. Canadian hiring managers specifically look for candidates who can quantify their work because it signals analytical thinking — the core skill in most tech and data roles.

### The AI Prompt to Fix It

> Here are my current resume bullet points for [job title] at [company]:
>
> [paste your current bullet points]
>
> Here is the job description I am targeting: [paste job description]
>
> Please rewrite each bullet point to:
>
> 1. Start with a strong, specific action verb (not "responsible for", "helped with", or "worked on")
> 2. Include measurable impact where possible (numbers, percentages, time saved, revenue, scale)
> 3. Match the seniority level and language of the job description
> 4. Be concise — one line each, maximum two lines for complex achievements
>
> Show me the original and rewritten version side by side.

### What Good Looks Like

Before: "Responsible for managing data pipelines."

After: "Designed and maintained 12 automated data pipelines processing 2M+ records daily, reducing manual intervention by 80%."

Before: "Helped the team with reporting."

After: "Built weekly executive dashboards in Power BI that reduced report generation time from 4 hours to 20 minutes."

## Mistake 3 — Missing Keywords (ATS Mismatch)

### What It Looks Like

You list "data pipelines" but the job description says "ETL pipelines." You list "dashboards" but they want "Power BI dashboards." You list "cloud" but they want "Azure."

These are the same skills. But ATS systems are not intelligent — they do literal keyword matching.

### Why It Gets You Rejected

Research consistently shows that 70 to 75% of resumes are rejected by ATS before a human ever sees them. The most common reason is keyword mismatch. This is especially true at Canadian banks and large enterprises that receive hundreds of applications per role.

### The AI Prompt to Fix It

> Here is my resume: [paste full resume text]
>
> Here is the job description I am applying to: [paste full job description]
>
> Please do the following:
>
> 1. Identify every technical skill, tool, methodology, and qualification mentioned in the job description
> 2. Cross-reference with my resume and list every keyword that is missing or phrased differently
> 3. For each missing keyword, suggest exactly where in my resume I could add it naturally (only where I genuinely have that experience)
> 4. Flag any skills I have listed that are not relevant to this role
>
> Format the output as a table with columns: Missing Keyword, Suggested Location, Priority (High, Medium, Low)

### Quick Wins to Look For

Tool names: "Power BI" vs "PowerBI" vs "Microsoft Power BI" — use the exact casing from the job description.

Role titles: If the JD says "Analytics Engineer" and your resume says "Data Engineer", consider whether your experience maps and update accordingly.

Methodologies: Agile, Scrum, CI/CD, dbt, ETL — often listed in JDs and missing from resumes even when candidates have the experience.

## 5 Bonus Prompts

### Bonus Prompt 1 — Cover Letter Opening Paragraph

> I am applying for [role] at [company]. Here is my resume: [paste resume]. Here is the job description: [paste JD].
>
> Write only the opening paragraph of a cover letter. It should reference something specific about this company (a recent announcement, product, or initiative) and connect it directly to my background. Do not start with "I am writing to apply for."

### Bonus Prompt 2 — LinkedIn Headline

> Here is my current LinkedIn headline: [paste headline]
>
> Here is my background: [2 sentences]
>
> I am targeting [type of role] at [type of company] in Canada.
>
> Write 5 alternative LinkedIn headlines that are specific, keyword-rich, and clearly communicate my value proposition. Each should be under 220 characters.

### Bonus Prompt 3 — Interview Prep Questions

> Here is the job description I am interviewing for: [paste JD]
>
> Here is my resume: [paste resume]
>
> Generate the 10 most likely interview questions for this specific role. For each question provide a brief framework for how I should structure my answer based on my background.

### Bonus Prompt 4 — Salary Negotiation Script

> I have received a job offer for [role] at [company] in Canada with a base salary of [amount]. My research suggests the market range is [range] for this role in [city]. My experience level is [X years].
>
> Write a short script I can use to negotiate salary over email or phone. Keep it professional, confident, and collaborative.

### Bonus Prompt 5 — Career Pivot Framing

> I am transitioning from [current field] to [target field]. Here is my resume: [paste resume]. Here is the job description I am targeting: [paste JD].
>
> Identify the top 5 transferable skills from my current experience that are most relevant to this new role. For each one suggest how to reframe it on my resume to resonate with hiring managers in the new field.

## Common Mistakes When Using AI for Resumes

Do not accept the first output without editing. AI language is often generic — your voice and specific numbers make the difference.

Do not fabricate experience. Only add keywords and skills you genuinely have.

Do not use the same resume for every application. Run the keyword prompt fresh for each role.

Do not let AI write your entire resume from scratch. Use it to improve specific sections. A fully AI-written resume reads like one.
